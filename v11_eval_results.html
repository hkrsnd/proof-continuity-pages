Experiment Overview

RAILS (Reasoning with Automatically Integrated Logical Supervision) trains LLMs to produce faithful reasoning by providing symbolic verification rewards during GRPO reinforcement learning. The key idea: a SAT solver checks whether each reasoning step is logically consistent with prior steps. Models receive reward only when their reasoning chain is both correct and logically valid — preventing reward hacking through unfaithful shortcuts.

Pipeline: Base model → SFT warmup on gold proofs → GRPO with either (a) answer-only reward or (b) answer + consistency reward. All models use 4-bit QLoRA. Evaluation spans 4 in-distribution benchmarks (LogicBench, FOLIO, ProntoQA, ProofWriter) and 2 transfer benchmarks (SARA, LSAT).

Key Findings

Consistency reward produces more faithful reasoning. Qwen3 +Consistency achieves 24.2% faithfulness vs 17.9% for Answer-Only — a +35% relative improvement. The consistency signal teaches models to reason validly, not just reach the right answer.
Answer-only training collapses reasoning quality. Qwen3 AnsOnly ProofWriter consistency drops to 4.8% (from 45.1% at SFT), and ProntoQA consistency to 9.8% (from 65.6%). The model learns to shortcut past genuine reasoning when only final answers are rewarded.
The accuracy-faithfulness tradeoff. Qwen3 AnsOnly achieves the highest LogicBench accuracy (50.5%) but through unfaithful shortcuts (17.9% faithfulness). Qwen3 +Consistency trades some accuracy (40.8%) for substantially more valid reasoning (24.2% faithfulness). This is the core RAILS insight: raw accuracy masks reasoning quality.
Consistency model wins on FOLIO — the hardest transfer benchmark. Qwen3 +Consistency achieves 51.0% on FOLIO vs 48.5% for AnsOnly. FOLIO requires complex FOL reasoning not seen during training, suggesting the consistency signal helps genuine generalization.
DeepSeek +Consistency wins on ProntoQA. DS +Consistency reaches 44.0% vs 40.3% for AnsOnly (+9.2% relative). The structured reasoning encouraged by consistency reward transfers well to chain-style deductive reasoning.
DeepSeek models show limited LogicBench improvement. All DS variants score 3–4% on LogicBench overall, suggesting the model may need more training time or a different SFT warmup strategy. The base DeepSeek-R1-7B architecture may be less amenable to the current format. Results on other benchmarks (FOLIO 33%, ProntoQA 44%, PW 49%) are much stronger.
Transfer benchmarks (SARA, LSAT) remain challenging. All models score below 7% on SARA and below 4% on LSAT. These benchmarks require domain knowledge beyond what the current training data provides.
