Key Findings

Transfer advantage: The consistency model is the only one that transfers to ProntoQA (30.3% vs ~0% for both others) and wins on FOLIO (56.4% vs 47.5% SFT, 46.6% answer-only). Models trained with consistency reward learn reasoning patterns that generalize beyond the training distribution.
Per-theory consistency improvement: Consistency training improved consistency rate in 16/25 LogicBench theories over the SFT baseline. Largest gains: disjunctive syllogism (+0.44), existential generalization (+0.29), modus tollens (+0.12), default reasoning (+0.14).
Answer-only shortcuts: The answer-only model achieves 72.5% LogicBench accuracy but with only 4.9 steps per example (vs 20.7 for consistency). It learned to compress disjunctions into single literals (e.g., encoding "A or not B" as just "A"), making proofs trivially verifiable but semantically unfaithful.
Accuracy gap: Consistency model's LogicBench accuracy (30.0%) barely improved over SFT (28.8%). The model produces longer, more careful reasoning but struggles to convert valid chains into correct final answers. Training was only 24% complete — this may improve with more training.
Overthinking on complex rules: On dilemma-type theories, the consistency model produces 26-34 step traces that loop and fail to converge, while the answer-only model's 5-step shortcuts land the right answer. The consistency reward creates a harder optimization landscape that needs more training to navigate.
ProofWriter consistency: Answer-only's ProofWriter consistency is near zero (0.032) — it learned to skip reasoning entirely. Consistency model maintains 0.375, and SFT baseline achieves 0.463. The answer-only model's "proofs" are structurally empty on out-of-distribution benchmarks.
